So there I was, working in the office when my stomach informed me it was time to feed the monster that lived inside my belly, and I made my way to the cafeteria. Neatly taking everything and putting it in its right place to maintain the equilibrium of the thali being served. 

I sat around the corner and was about to start eating when someone whispered in my ear, “You forgot to start *Taarak Mehta Ka Ooltah Chashma*.” Now, sure, I had not slept well last night, so it might just have been my sleep paralysis demon, but hey, the point was correct. Any meal was incomplete without having something to watch. 

That something always took the shape of *Taarak Mehta*, and that’s when I realised just how bad television had gotten—and this deterioration in quality takes shape in both the content and its presentation.

### The Death of the Creative Commercial

Let’s start with presentation. Tell me the last time an ad break genuinely made you notice the product or had you hooked from start to end? To me, it was the time of CRED ads or, more recently, the SuperMoney app. And this is my recollection from 2020 to now, and that to me seems really bad. 

I remember in the good old days (can’t believe being born in 2004 and still using the phrase “good old days,” peak Bhide vibes) when I would be glued to the screen whenever an ad for Motilal Oswal, smooth pens, chocolates, or energy drinks came up, and I remembered each and every detail. But now the focus is less on the ads themselves and more on the corner, waiting with bated breath for when the angel of "skip" will come and save me from the abhorrent state of ads that are currently plaguing India.

### A Stagnant Creative Landscape

The next issue is with the content. Indian television doesn’t take risks nowadays; we used to, though. There were shows like *Satyameva Jayate*, or comedic masterpieces like *Sarabhai v/s Sarabhai*, that tackled nuanced topics. There was something for everyone, and these were groundbreaking developments in Indian television that should have resulted in the momentum being carried forward by the new generation of creators. Alas, it was not meant to be. 

In modern times, the only show that I can think of that actually tried to do something would probably be *Sacred Games*. Being the first Netflix Original Indian series, it had a lot riding on it. Though failing in the latter half, it did succeed in making people take notice of this new form of storytelling that India was trying to create. But now, the problem that has plagued television is that there is no new innovation taking place as to what the medium can achieve.

In India, the television scene can broadly be divided into four repetitive categories:

1. **The "Sitcoms" / Dramas:** There are no actual sitcoms; they are mostly drama shows with a few comedic elements spread throughout.
2. **Mythological Shows:** Endless reproductions of epics and folklore.
3. **Reality Shows:** Highly orchestrated talent and drama competitions.
4. **The IP-Galore:** Low-effort sci-fi and supernatural fantasy shows that keep getting sequels (the usual culprits being *Naagin*, *Baalveer*, and such).

And that’s it. You will never find a show that will ever try to expand beyond the confines of these genres.

### The OTT Trap: False Promises and Sequel Greed

The same issue plagues the OTT scene as well, where the shows fall into one of two traps:

* **Great Concept, Terrible Execution:** Projects like *Betaal* that hold incredible promise but fall apart structurally.
* **Producer Greed:** Great shows where the relentless urge for monetization makes their content run into the ground through unnecessary seasons (shows like *Mirzapur*, *The Family Man*, and *Inside Edge*).

The root problem is that they are not creating anything fundamentally original or something that you have never seen before. It’s basically borrowing concepts from western media and giving them a localized Indian spin. The novelty runs out real quick. How applying cheese to everything was all the rage, but now look at us; the journey from cheese to chia seeds has truly been a journey.

### Conclusion

So, with the content being bad and the breaks in between being entirely forgettable, what hope does Indian television have? 

In my humble opinion, it’s just that the masses will have to outright reject mediocrity. You can already see that change happening in cinema, where mediocre comedy films and stale spy adventures are no longer something that audiences blindly buy tickets for. Let’s just hope that the TV and OTT-watching audience can eventually do the same.
