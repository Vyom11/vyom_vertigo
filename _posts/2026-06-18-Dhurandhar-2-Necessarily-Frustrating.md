A new day dawns upon Bollywood, where our grandparents had *Mughal-E-Azam*, our parents had *Sholay*, and we will have *Dhurandhar* as the defining film of this generation. And the reason for this is quite simple—it’s just that good of a film.

> **SPOILERS AHEAD** for *Dhurandhar: The Revenge* and *Dhurandhar*.

So, a bit of background: I enjoyed *Dhurandhar: The Revenge* (which I’ll be referring to as *D2* for the rest of the article’s length because the procrastinator at heart is at work), whereas I loved *Dhurandhar*. I was there first day, first show for *Dhurandhar*, and I knew that we had a gem on our hands. I vividly remember the blood-red background, those harrowing screams, those aura-farming moments, and the gritty nature of Lyari. Where the first movie excelled in the exterior, it’s the introspective nature of *D2* that pulls me into the heart of Jaskirat.

### The Facade vs. The Reality

The first movie is all about facade—how every single character has a mask on themselves. Starting with Hamza, it’s obvious from the onset that he’s not who he says he is. Then it’s the other characters, like Aalam Bhai, who just longs for familiar faces, places, and the feeling of belonging—where he would have longed for the taste of kulfi falooda but is left with doodh soda. 

Or how someone like Rehman might act suave, but deep down, it’s the fear of losing control that doesn’t let him rest in peace. How Aslam might act macho, dancing to the tune of “Hawa Hawa,” but detests the people above him and still keeps doing their bidding because he has to maintain that mask. Or how Jamali is the most consistent in maintaining the mask of a cunningly jolly guy (the reasons are made clear in the next part, but the insinuation of his real nature is always kept at bay).

Contrast that with the second part, where the masks are taken away and a picture is drawn: those who are made stronger by the past are the ones who come out on top, whereas those who give in to the past are left with nothing but the ashes of a forgotten and lost future. 

### The Weight of a Conscience

Nowhere is that clearer than in the face-off between Jaskirat and Iqbal. Jaskirat, who had his innocence taken away by the very system that was supposed to protect him; how the one he trusted to lead his family to a better future is the one who betrays him (and how, rightfully, he’s the first one to “uncover” the reality of who Hamza is); how, even after going through every dirty deed just so that information about an attack is sent to the right sources, he’s still unable to protect his nation. These failures do live in his conscience, but it’s the thoughts of Gita and Guru Granth Sahib that make it so that he keeps pushing forward.

Contrast that with Iqbal, who, apart from being very clearly evil, is still shown as somewhat of a family man. But it’s only after that barrier is breached that he drowns his father and buries whatever good he had. This leads me to the point where many said that having the role of Iqbal’s father in the movie weakened him. 

To that, I say—that was the point. The one we already considered a threat in the first part is shown as nothing but a jester in his own house. It’s only after he kills his father and shaves his head that he truly becomes the Angel of Death. It’s the reason why we are given clear titles for others—how “Bastard King of Lyari” is Rehman and how Aslam is “The Jinn”—but the title “Angel of Death” only comes after he has been “cleaned.”

Now, what I would have liked personally is to go deeper in different directions—how it was necessary to spend more time in Jaskirat’s heart (that opening sequence is probably the best shot and best-acted portion of the entire film), and we needed to spend more time in Iqbal’s mind to rebuild him as a formidable foe. But that would have resulted in the creation of *Dhurandhar 3: The Conclusion*, which, no doubt, we would have loved. Still, I appreciate the restraint shown by Dhar.

### The Power of Somber Moments

That leads me to the second point where I feel *D2* excels—in the somber moments. I cheered as much as the next guy when Jaskirat pulls out the rocket launcher, but I feel it’s the crying face of young Jaskirat that will have a lasting impact on me. 

Hearing him listen to Yalina saying his real name—where throughout the entire runtime of the film, that name has stuck as a pebble on his feet, but it’s only after hearing it through her that he feels some sense of normalcy and calm in a land where he doesn’t really belong—and seeing his family not happy but content, is incredible. Rather than showing a happy reunion, it gives the audience a chance to ponder, letting them think whether Jaskirat breaks the illusion or lets it remain. It certainly was one of the most impactful frames of Bollywood for me—and for many others, I’m sure.

Coming to the story aspect, this is the biggest hurdle that I knew *D2* would never be able to surpass its predecessor in. While the first part was smaller in scale but bigger in impact, this tries to be bigger in scale but loses out on impact. The reason is simple: it’s easier for us to believe that Jaskirat can join a gang and kill Rehman, but a bit far-fetched that he was able to do so much damage in *D2* with little to no obstacles, with just the help of Rizwan and Mr. Blinkit Shirani. This is where I feel it would have been a better decision to keep the training montage earlier and show that it wasn’t just Rizwan and Jaskirat who did it, rather the entire team of Balidan.

Now again, this is not against the movie in any sense—this is just me expressing how I would have liked the film to be. Dhar has very honestly stated in the title what the movie is about—it’s the Revenge. That’s what he told you, and that’s what he delivered. And the only reason the film is celebrated is because of its third act, where even though the first act seems slow and the second act a bit too convenient, it’s the emotional impact of the third act that makes the story well worth the effort. As rightly said, you must leave the audience with a good dessert so they may forgive the less-than-perfect main course.

### The "Propaganda" Debate

Moving further into the story, many have said that the *Dhurandhar* saga is propaganda. To that I say—of course it is. While the first one was understated in its commentary, here it’s made very clear the stand Dhar has taken politically. Honestly, I have no qualms about it, as it doesn't ruin the filmmaking experience—considering you take the film as a fictional story based on real events. 

If you start considering *Dhurandhar* as your source of information, then there’s a reason why the disclaimer was attached before the film started—for people like you. And to be fair, it’s his money and his ideas; he can share them however he likes. Every movie has an agenda—some ideas they want to project. You can’t be that guy who says *"left kare toh Kung Fu Panda aur right kare toh propaganda."* And even if you feel that way, you have to admit that it’s well-made propaganda.

### Technical Mastery

Getting into the “well-made” aspect, the *Dhurandhar* saga excels in the technical department. Very rarely do you see such grip in directing, where the biggest meme is about the peak detailing done by the director. Apart from that, it’s the cinematography, set design, and production design that make the world of the movie feel real and lived-in. 

Another aspect is the casting, which seems as if Chhabra saab went to hell and back to ensure these people felt as real as possible. Apart from that, it’s the soundtrack and score that deserve a special mention. Very rarely (probably never) have I been truly blown away by the soundtrack and background score of a Bollywood film—making it so that the Aditya-Shashwat duo has truly become a force to be reckoned with.

### The Verdict

So, all in all, how is *Dhurandhar*? Well, it’s necessarily frustrating, as for the first time, Indian audiences are thirsty for more—thirsty for better content. It feels genuinely good to see an actually well-made film get showered with love and praise (unlike mediocre films like *Chhava* or the aesthetic Bihari-fication of films like *Pushpa 2*). And even if you disagree, it feels great to see a movie be the talking point of the nation, where everyone at least appreciates the blood, sweat, and tears that went into making this project a reality.

This is very much an *Avengers: Infinity War*–*Endgame* situation, where the first part is technically and structurally the better film, but it’s the second one that forms the complete package. If you consider both these films as meant to be watched together, you would find the idea of *D2* much more appreciative. You can feel the rising tension where seeing Jaskirat get beaten up in the dirty lanes of Lyari in the first scene to him getting to see the beautiful land of Pathankot feels like a natural progression. The upbeat nature of the first film's soundtrack beautifully contrasts with the more understated and somber soundtrack of *D2* which grows on you over time. 

I certainly would have liked to see the saga expand further, but the way Ajay told Jaskirat that he did good, we too can collectively tell Aditya—good. Time for the saga and the talent involved to revel in the success of their mission, *Dhurandhar*.
