AI art is all the rage in the world today. Some people view it as a threat to creativity and human expression, while others celebrate it as a revolutionary technological breakthrough. Where do I stand in all of this? I find myself conflicted. While I appreciate the possibilities AI offers, I also feel that the way it is currently built and deployed can sometimes diminish the value of the human effort that lies behind artistic creation.

Why do I feel this way? Because I have spent years on both sides of this conversation. I currently work in artificial intelligence and machine learning, while also carrying more than a decade of experience in artistic pursuits through painting and theatre.

To understand my concerns, we first need to understand what AI-generated art actually is. Image and video generation tools do not create works in the same way human beings do. Rather, they produce outputs by learning patterns from enormous amounts of existing material and combining those patterns in ways that appear coherent and meaningful.

Let’s take an example: say I ask an AI to create an image of an astronaut in the form of a Raja Ravi Varma painting. The model is not imagining an astronaut the way a person might. Instead, it draws upon everything it has learned about astronauts, Raja Ravi Varma, portraiture, colour palettes, and composition. It then blends those learned patterns into an output that appears convincing at first glance (as seen in the image below). Yet the longer you look at it, the more questions begin to emerge.

![Astronaut in the style of Raja Ravi Varma](https://vyomvertigo.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/image.png?allow_lossy=1)

Why was this image generated in this particular way? To answer that, it helps to look at some of the assumptions embedded within the output itself.

Why would an astronaut pose for a portrait in what appears to be a random outdoor setting? Most astronaut portraits are taken in environments connected to space exploration. Yet Raja Ravi Varma often painted his subjects against natural or outdoor backdrops, and so the AI gravitates toward that aesthetic.

Why do we see the NASA logo alongside the Indian flag? If Raja Ravi Varma were painting an astronaut today, one might reasonably expect an association with ISRO. However, because images of astronauts in training data are overwhelmingly American, the model leans toward NASA as its default representation of space travel.

Similarly, without any additional prompting, the model generated a brown male with a moustache. This reflects patterns within the data it has absorbed regarding what an Indian astronaut is expected to look like. Biases of this nature have been observed repeatedly in machine learning systems. For example, models trained on historical data often associate men with professions such as doctors and women with professions such as nurses, simply because those associations were more common in the data they learned from.

The more images you generate, the more you begin to notice another pattern. AI systems tend to produce outputs that sit near the centre of what they have learned. By this, I mean that they often generate content that feels familiar, safe, and statistically likely. You can certainly adjust parameters to make outputs more creative or unpredictable, but pushing too far in either direction frequently results in images that become incoherent or detached from the prompt altogether.

At this point, an important question emerges. If AI generates images by drawing upon existing knowledge of astronauts and Raja Ravi Varma, where does that knowledge come from?

The answer is straightforward: it comes from data collected from human-created works. Many artists, writers, photographers, and creators have raised concerns that their work has been used in training datasets without meaningful consent, compensation, or attribution. Earlier image-generation systems struggled because they lacked sufficient training material. Today’s systems are far more capable precisely because they have been trained on vastly larger collections of creative work.

This is the aspect of AI art that I believe deserves greater scrutiny. These systems are not creating something from nothing. They are generating outputs from patterns found in existing works. If an AI had never encountered information about astronauts or Raja Ravi Varma, it would have no meaningful basis from which to generate the image we requested. At best, it would produce something nonsensical. So when someone says, “I created this Ghibli image of myself,” it is worth remembering that such an image is only possible because the model learned from the work of countless artists whose contributions made that output achievable in the first place.

## The Fire We Chose to Share

Now, does that mean every person who uses AI tools is acting irresponsibly? I do not think so.

The story of Prometheus offers an interesting lens through which to view this debate. In Greek mythology, Prometheus gave fire to humanity. That gift allowed people to cook food, forge tools, build industries, and create wonders that would have been unimaginable otherwise. Yet the same fire could also destroy homes and harm communities. The tool itself was neither wholly good nor wholly bad; its impact depended on how people chose to use it.

AI feels remarkably similar.

There are many areas where AI genuinely helps creators. Tasks that once consumed hours of repetitive labour can now be completed in minutes. In animation, for example, processes such as rotoscoping can be significantly accelerated through machine-learning-assisted workflows. In such cases, AI acts as a tool that removes friction and allows artists to focus more of their energy on creative decision-making.

My discomfort arises when the tool begins replacing the creative process itself rather than supporting it. Generating an image in the style of a living artist may feel harmless to the user, but from the perspective of the artist, it can feel as though years of study, experimentation, and personal expression have been reduced to a button press. The concern is not simply about technology; it is about whether we continue to value the people whose work made these systems possible.

There is also a broader cost to consider. Modern AI systems require enormous computational infrastructure. The data centres that train and run these models consume significant amounts of energy, water, and resources. While society celebrates the convenience of these tools, the environmental and economic costs are often pushed into the background.

Meanwhile, the companies building these systems rarely face the same scrutiny as the creators whose work forms part of their training data. Legal challenges from writers, artists, and publishers have begun to question whether existing practices around data collection are fair, but many of these debates remain unresolved. Regardless of where the courts eventually land, the ethical questions are unlikely to disappear anytime soon.

Supporters of AI often point out that users voluntarily agree to terms and conditions when interacting with these platforms. Technically, this is true. Yet there is a meaningful difference between legal consent and informed consent. Most people do not spend hours reading lengthy legal documents before clicking "accept." As a result, many users remain unaware of how their data may be stored, analysed, or incorporated into future systems.

Whether one views this as acceptable or not, it highlights a larger challenge: technological progress often moves much faster than public understanding. The result is a growing gap between what these systems can do and what people believe they are agreeing to when they use them.

## The Promise and Limits of Accessible Creativity

One of the most common arguments in favour of AI-generated art is that it democratises creativity. The claim is simple: by lowering technical barriers, more people can express themselves creatively.

There is certainly some truth to this idea. Someone with no drawing experience can now generate images that would have been impossible for them to create independently. That accessibility can be exciting and empowering.

At the same time, I think creativity is about more than producing an end result.

Learning to paint, write, act, animate, compose music, or make films is not merely a means to obtaining a finished product. The process itself shapes the person creating it. Every brushstroke reflects a decision. Every performance reflects countless hours of practice. Every film carries traces of the worldview, experiences, and perspective of the people who made it.

Art derives much of its meaning from that journey.

When a person spends years developing a craft, they are not simply acquiring a skill. They are learning how to observe the world differently. They are discovering how to communicate emotions, ideas, and experiences that would otherwise remain unspoken.

That is why I sometimes worry that conversations about AI art focus too heavily on outputs while overlooking the value of the creative process itself.

It is also why I find it bittersweet to see talented filmmakers and artists experimenting with fully AI-generated productions. The technology can undoubtedly produce visually impressive results. Yet visual quality alone does not necessarily create emotional depth. What often resonates most in art is the presence of another human being behind the work—the sense that someone experienced something, felt something, and chose to share it with us.

A generated image may replicate the appearance of a brushstroke. A generated performance may imitate an expression. But imitation and experience are not the same thing. My concern is not that AI will destroy art. Human beings have always adapted to new technologies. Rather, my concern is that we may gradually lose sight of what made artistic expression meaningful in the first place.

## What AI Should Be For

Despite my criticisms, I do not believe AI is inherently harmful. In fact, I believe it can become one of the most useful creative tools ever developed.

When used thoughtfully, AI can remove repetitive labour, accelerate workflows, improve accessibility, and help creators spend more time on genuinely creative work. In that role, it serves as an assistant rather than a replacement.

What I struggle with is the growing tendency to equate generating an artwork with creating one. The distinction may seem small, but it matters. Art is not only the final image, film, or song. It is also the accumulation of choices, experiences, failures, revisions, and discoveries that led to its creation.

As AI continues to evolve, I hope we find ways to embrace its strengths without forgetting the people whose creativity laid the foundation for it. Technology should expand human expression, not diminish our appreciation for it.

Because if innovation is measured only by convenience, efficiency, and shareholder value, then we risk overlooking something far more important: the human stories, skills, and struggles that make art worth creating in the first place.
