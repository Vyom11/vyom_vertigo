AI art is all the rage in the world, where some people are calling it one of the apostles of death, while some are professing it as the second coming of Christ. Where do I stand in all of this? Well, I feel that AI, being a capitalist pig, is more so an insult to humanity.

How can I say this? Well, because I have been at both fronts of technology and art. Currently working my way through artificial intelligence and machine learning, while also having a decade worth of experience in creating art (in the form of both paintings and theatre).

So, to begin with the core of evil, we must first understand evil. In this case, that evil takes shape and calls itself AI. Now, image and video generation tools provided by AI are nothing but a mix-match of existing properties to create a meaningful output. 

Let’s take an example: say I ask an AI to create an image of an astronaut in the form of a Raja Ravi Varma painting. The model is not actually creating something; all it is doing is looking into its learnings and picking out the different content it deems necessary to the question the user has asked (in our case, that is the requirement to create an image), which in turn it merges to give you an output that seems fine on the surface (as seen in the image below), but the more you ponder on the content shown, some questions and intrigue pops up.

![Astronaut in the style of Raja Ravi Varma](https://vyomvertigo.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/image.png?allow_lossy=1)

Now, why was this image generated in the way it was? Well, first let’s take a look at the problems that make it so the image does deliver on what I asked for, but it doesn’t really make much sense.

Why would an astronaut take their photo at a random place (considering all astronauts get their photos clicked in places related to space)? But that is not the case because Raja Ravi Varma had his paintings painted in similar outward places, so the output feels that way.

Why is it that we see the logo of NASA, yet the Indian flag? Shouldn’t an astronaut drawn by Raja Ravi Varma be from ISRO? That is because the dataset present for astronauts is predominantly American, resulting in the output being of preference to NASA.

Similarly, you can see that, without describing the details of who the astronaut is, the model generated a brown male with a moustache because that is the data being present in the model of who an Indian astronaut should look like (this is a very common problem wherein you give the model to identify whether a person is a doctor or nurse by giving it the name of a girl and a boy. Nine times out of ten, you would see that the model has given the occupation of nurse to a girl and the boy being given the occupation of doctor).

And the more you generate images (I do not advocate it), you will start to see a pattern where the output is squarely in the median of what AI is capable of. Now, what do I mean by median? In the case of content generation, it will always give you an output that is the most normal thing and the most inoffensive stuff ever generated (sure, you can change the temperature of the model to make it more creative or sterile), but that does not change the idea that changing such settings results in the model going off the rails to create an output that is incomprehensible and a bunch of gibberish.

The question that you must ask yourselves by this point in time is that, when explaining how an image gets generated, I told you that the model looks into itself to take a look at existing data of astronauts and Raja Ravi Varma. Where does it get the data from? The answer is simple, really. Companies like Anthropic and OpenAI steal artists’ work without crediting them to expand upon their database. This is why earlier image generation models were criticised because they did not have enough data to produce meaningful output; now they do.

This is the part that people fail to understand: AI and its models are not actually creating something from nothing. They are creating something from existing things. If you gave an AI no data to work on, say you do not explain to it what an astronaut is and who Raja Ravi Varma was, then it won’t be able to give you an output, or even if it did (as we all know how much our models love to treat us as emperors), it would be utter nonsense. So, when you say that, “I created this Ghibli image of myself,” be very aware of the fact that that image was based off stealing countless artworks of actual artists.

### The Fire and the Burning House

Now, are you at fault in all this? To a certain extent, yes, but mostly no. When, in Greek mythology, Prometheus gave fire to humans, those very humans created cooked food, forged industries, and made marvels never seen before with the help of those very embers, alongside which you also saw ritualistic burnings and houses being burnt down for residing people deemed worthless. Keep in mind that this exchange resulted in Prometheus being punished as well by having his liver eaten every day and humans being given Pandora’s Box. 

Now apply that same principle in mind, and you start to see a disparity, where AI is actually helping people in many fields (art included). How earlier rotoscoping (an animation technique where artists trace over live-action video footage frame-by-frame) would take hours to do manually now takes a few minutes with the advent of AI, and that is actual usage of the fire, while creating an AI image of yourself in the style of Van Gogh or Raja Ravi Varma is like burning the house of the artist because you needed warmth to feel comfortable and good about yourself. 

The punishment is the usage of huge amounts of energy and resources to run these data centres and keep these machines running. Now, you might have noticed that in the original Greek story, even Prometheus is punished. But in today’s world, these megalomaniacal maniacs run scot-free and walk haughty and proud of themselves for giving such power to us. They don’t face any repercussions for their actions (let alone getting their liver eaten every single day), though that is fortunately changing as these companies had to pay writers for plagiarising and using their work without their consent (the final judgement is still pending).

Now, that was from the perspective of someone who loathes AI art. Some might argue on the other side that these companies are backed by major corporations, for example how Anthropic (the company behind Claude) has the backing of Amazon and Alphabet, or how Gemini is built by Google, where in their terms and conditions it might be written that the intellectual property is yours, but you grant permission to these companies to use, host, store, or modify your data as deemed necessary. To that, I say balderdash, as there is no way in hell and back that a user would read these terms and conditions, and just because it is written doesn’t make it morally correct. But sure, if these companies use these arguments to justify stealing, then sure, let them have their day.

### The Illusion of Democratic Creativity

Another reasoning you would hear these “AI artists” talk about is to say that it opens the floodgates for people to be creative, and to that I say, NO. Like, first of all, it’s only image generation that is free. Video generation is hella expensive, so it doesn't open any gates so that a common person can create AI-generated movies, let alone clips. 

And about image generation, wouldn’t it give you more joy to actually learn a skill than asking someone else to do the work for you? Because let me state it clearly: art as a medium finds its purpose not by its final output, but with the efforts and experiences of the people that shaped the artwork. The paintings are the result of what a person felt during each brushstroke, a film is a reflection of the worldview of a creator, and gorgeous cinematography is the result of an artist understanding what makes a shot look great. 

It saddens me to see great creators like Rahi Anil Barve (director of *Tumbbad*) and Darren Aronofsky (director of *Black Swan*) jump on the bandwagon to create AI movies when, in return, they don’t get an actor with expressions and nuance behind their eyes, just an empty void of nothingness—a void so deep that it won’t just bring about a ruin to the craft of art, rather it would lead to a fundamental backward change in the understanding of what art means to so many people.

### Conclusion

I feel AI, as a supporting tool, is genuinely needed. It takes the burden of manual work away from actual artists so they can focus on true creation. However, creating complete art pieces from scratch using AI and then calling yourself an artist is, frankly, insulting. 

But hey, if a product creates shareholder value and keeps the average citizen aloof, then that’s apparently all that’s needed in the 21st century…
