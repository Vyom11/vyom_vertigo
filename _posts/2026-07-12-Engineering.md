# What Engineering Actually Teaches You

I know this website is meant for movie discussions, but trust me, this title will make sense by the time you finish this article.

Do engineering. If not academically, then as a hobby—but learn engineering. It could be anything: electrical, mechanical, civil, computer—anything at all. The reason for this is quite simple: doing engineering helps you expand your worldview in ways unimaginable.

And I say this as an engineer working as a writer, with no bias. Which sounds hard to believe. A guy spewing thoughts on the domain he has graduated from must be biased, right? But no. I really had thought that maybe my studies were meant to be in the arts. Back during the COVID days, when I had just finished my 12th exams after getting amazingly bad scores in JEE, I distinctly remember getting a score of -1 in Maths and a rank of around 10,00,000. So I really did consider going into the arts, either literature or filmmaking, to try and make a career there. But alas, destiny had written that, as an Indian male who had finished PCM, I must go into engineering. And so I did.

And let me tell you something (I say this the way Jon Bernthal meant it). Those four years of my life were some of the best I have ever had. Not because of the learnings—no, no, absolutely not—but because they opened up so many doors for me and gave me a bit of leeway that I did not expect. Being great at public speaking in a field where people considered you a nerd was a blessing in disguise for me.

I remember when I had gone to Ganpat University for the 38th AIU Inter-University West Zone UniFest 2025 as an elocution candidate, and everyone underestimated me when I mentioned to them that I was doing engineering. The kids from the arts scoffed, thinking, "How dare this peasant come under my glory as a royal of the speaking arts?" And mind you, I did not correct them by saying that I had experience on stage ever since I was a wee little child. And lo and behold, when my name was announced for first position, the look on their faces was a feast for the eyes.

Apart from this easy access to events through engineering, the other benefit I had was exposure to practically all forms of information available. I was aware of what LLM model was being launched while also taking a look at what new Criterion discs were being released. This multifaceted knowledge helped me become a jack of all trades and a master of some, which would not have been possible had I gone into the arts. I could study computer networks, cryptography, and AIML in the morning, handle an event as a university representative in the evening, and watch series or play Valorant with my roommates late into the night. That is the experience I got during those four years.

Engineering will not help you become a smarter person, per se, but it will help you become a better person. One instance where doing engineering will help you is not in college, but after you come out of college. Those moments when a person with a single area of expertise struggles with forces outside their domain while you sail through them with ease—that is when you will think, "Damn, I guess engineering did help me out."

The other instance where engineering helps is with the people you meet. Now, outliers always exist, but I have seen that people in medicine don't really have a life outside of it (they are preparing to save lives, so I understand), and people from the arts act so haughty and proud that it's no wonder they can't see beyond the confines of what their curriculum tells them to think about.

That is why I feel that engineering is maybe the only field where you can actually meet people who are as different as the types of toppings in Indian fast food. To give a few examples, I know a great writer and dancer who did IT engineering. I know a guy who was an avid filmmaker while doing electrical engineering. I knew a great photographer who was studying computer engineering. I knew a magician and a cartoonist who were doing IT. The same goes for singers, painters, and writers who were engineers. That kind of variety is something you can only get to see among people doing engineering.

The argument that gets presented is that if these kids had actually pursued their interests, they might have become great in their craft and would have been known worldwide. And to that, I say: No. Absolutely not.

Because sadly—and this is universal across the world—art doesn't pay shite. And with the proliferation of knowledge, you do not need to get a degree in these things to get going. But for engineering, you do. And it helps you gain a better understanding of both the technical side of your mind and the creative one (I know your brain is not actually divided into parts, but you get my point).

So, in conclusion, if you are the type of person who is confused about what to do, or you are passionate about everything, do engineering. It's not as difficult as people make it out to be, and it will benefit you in every possible way, whether in your professional life or your personal life. Or, if you are a creative at heart, trust me—learning engineering, even as a hobby, will help you see the world in ways you never thought were possible.

And at the end of the day, isn't that what a creative craves? A new way to look at the world. A different way to experience reality. An otherworldly feeling of accomplishment.

This is all engineering teaches you.
