# Cover > Content

Aesthetics matter. A film having a good story is not enough; it must look good as well. What fun is there in watching a movie if the pictures coming out of the screen are not good to look at? Cinema is, after all, a visual medium. But there is a particular idea that has begun to creep into the way we consume art: that if a film has good colour grading, dark undertones, neon lights and a vaguely symbolic atmosphere, then the film is automatically a point better than everyone else, even if the actual content would remind you of the quality of *War 2* or *Adipurush*.

You see this particularly among film snobs on Twitter and Instagram. A trailer comes out that is slightly darker than usual, someone walks through a corridor illuminated by red neon lights, and there is a shot of a glass of water that probably symbolises the protagonist's troubled relationship with his father. And suddenly, the film is artistic. Or, more accurately, *A24-esque*. Or perhaps it has a “Neon vibe”. And yes, I am aware that Neon is a film distributor and that the term is probably being used incorrectly. But I have seen enough people describe a film as having a “Neon vibe” to know that the phrase has now entered the cinematic vocabulary of people who want to sound like they have just returned from a film festival.

There is nothing wrong with a film looking good. In fact, a film should look good. But sometimes, the aesthetic becomes so powerful that it begins to substitute the thing it is supposed to enhance. I think about this every time I look at Nicholas Winding Refn's filmography. After *Drive*, his neon-soaked, hyper-stylised visual language became so culturally influential that it almost became a genre of its own. Neon lights, synth music, slow motion, violent men staring into the middle distance — suddenly, the visual language of *Drive* was everywhere.

But the interesting thing about *Drive* is that its style worked because there was something beneath it. The atmosphere was not the entire film. The aesthetic was not the point; it was one part of the experience. Eventually, we started copying the surface. The neon lights remained. The synthesiser remained. The slow motion remained. The colour grading remained. And somewhere along the way, the actual movie went missing. There is a reason why everyone remembers the song *Nightcall* and the opening of *Drive*, but no one remembers the plot of the film.

That, I think, is the larger problem. We have become very good at recognising the visual language of quality. We are less interested in checking whether the quality is actually there. We live in an age where the packaging is often more important than the product. You can share a beautiful image from a film. You can share a great-looking clip from a theatre. You can post photographs of a 70mm IMAX screening and make the experience look like you just witnessed Lisan-Al-Gaib. I would know. I cannot go to most of them because apparently enjoying cinema is now part of a club of exclusivity, but I digress.

Nobody, however, is going to share a great piece of dialogue. Nobody is going to post a screenshot of a paragraph and say, “Look at the construction of this sentence.” We are busy sharing memes, so kindly do not disturb us with your pedantic literature.

And this obsession with good packaging and bad products does not stop at cinema. It affects literature as well. There are Hindi novels and stories that contain some of the most profound writing you will ever read. But they are rarely discussed in the same cultural spaces, or with the same reverence, as their Western counterparts. Part of the reason is that presentation influences perception.

A beautifully designed English-language novel can immediately appear sophisticated, literary and important. It has a minimalist cover, carefully chosen typography and perhaps a single object placed against a beige background. You look at it and think: *This book will change my life.* Meanwhile, a Hindi book might have a solid-coloured cover, a straightforward title and a simple illustration. How are you supposed to look cool reading that? You have to be the performative type these days to warrant attention; simply being knowledgeable won't suffice.

And yes, that is a ridiculous way to judge literature. But people judge books by their covers all the time, literally and metaphorically. There is nothing inherently wrong with that. Good packaging means better visibility. Better visibility means a greater chance of someone picking up the product. That is how the system is supposed to work. A good cover should make you curious. A good poster should make you want to see the film. A good trailer should make you buy the ticket.

The problem begins when the packaging becomes enough.

Take energy drinks. People will happily drink Monster in public. Monster has aura. Monster has a personality. Monster looks like it could power a Formula 1 car, a metal concert and an extremely questionable decision. But there are people who will be embarrassed to be seen drinking Sting. They are both energy drinks. One is considered cool. The other is something you buy when you have ₹20 and no self-respect.

The product may be broadly similar, but the packaging creates an entirely different cultural perception. And that is what aesthetics do. They create expectations before we experience the actual thing.

Which is why social media can be so dangerous for the way we consume art. Someone posts a clip from a comedy film. The clip is genuinely funny. You watch it, the algorithm has done its job, and you think: *This looks hilarious.* So you watch the entire film. And then you realise that the scene you saw was the funniest scene in the entire movie. For example, I have seen so many reels for *Dilwale* where people reminisce about the Pogo, Ramlal and Kingo scene. And that is because those are the only two scenes that are funny in the 2-hour-38-minute film. The rest of the film is just the journey towards that scene, except you have already seen the destination.

The same thing happened to me with Dr Pepper and Vanilla Coke. I saw people online talking about them as if they were going to fundamentally alter my understanding of beverages. So I tried them. And instead of tasting like heaven, they tasted like cough syrup. A cough syrup I had paid ₹120 for. But fool me once, shame on me. I did not get fooled twice. The shame is still on me, but at least I can swallow my pride.

And perhaps that is the real problem with aesthetics. Aesthetics are supposed to create curiosity. But we have started treating them as evidence. A dark colour palette becomes evidence that a film is intelligent. A minimalist cover becomes evidence that a book is profound. A foreign title becomes evidence that a restaurant is expensive. A particular brand of energy drink becomes evidence that you have aura. A viral clip becomes evidence that the entire film is worth watching.

The aesthetic becomes the argument.

And sometimes, it works. Sometimes, the thing really is as good as it looks. But sometimes, you are simply staring at a beautifully designed box containing absolute nonsense.

The cover should make you pick up the book. The poster should make you buy the ticket. The packaging should make you curious. But eventually, the thing inside has to justify the reason you opened it.

Because there is a subtle art to doing covers, to making them look good enough that, after looking at them, you want to go out and do epic shit. But maybe try and read into them as well.

Aesthetics can make you try something you would otherwise ignore. They can make you pick up a book, buy a ticket, order a drink, or watch a film from a filmmaker you have never heard of. And that is powerful. But once the cover has done its job, the content has to take over.

So yes, aesthetics matter. They matter a lot. But maybe, before we decide that something is brilliant because it has neon lights, grain, a beautiful poster and a 70mm screening, we should check whether there is actually anything inside worth looking at.
